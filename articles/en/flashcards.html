The Brain Magic of Flashcards

McMinsky | 20 min read

No, not these cards. But they are also a trick.

The cards we are going to talk about are smaller, simpler, and infinitely more powerful than any poker hand. They are flashcards — cards with a question on one side and an answer on the other. And science tells us they are, quite probably, the most effective study tool ever invented.

This article is not another "top 5 study tips" piece. It is a serious deep dive: classical and modern science, techniques used by the best students in the world, fatal mistakes that nearly everyone makes, free tools, ready-to-use decks, and how artificial intelligence is transforming everything. Let us begin.

Classical science: why we forget

In 1885, German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus sat down and memorised hundreds of nonsense syllables. Then he measured how much he forgot over time. The result is one of the most brutal discoveries in psychology: we forget roughly 50% of what we learn within 30 minutes, and 70-80% within 24 hours. He called it the forgetting curve.

The good news? Ebbinghaus also discovered the antidote: each time we review the information, the curve flattens. The second review lasts longer than the first. The third, longer than the second. This is the spacing effect — one of the most replicated findings in the entire history of cognitive science.

In 1972, German science journalist Sebastian Leitner took this idea and created the Leitner Box: a system of boxes where cards we get right advance to boxes with longer review intervals, and cards we get wrong go back to box 1. Elegant in its simplicity — and devastatingly effective.

Modern science: why testing beats studying

In 2006, Henry Roediger and Jeffrey Karpicke of Washington University published a finding that should be hanging on every classroom wall in the world: testing yourself on material is more effective for long-term retention than studying the same material again. Not a little more effective — drastically more effective.

They called it the testing effect. In subsequent studies, students who practised active retrieval retained up to 50% more information after one week than students who simply re-read their notes.

Why? When we try to remember something, the brain strengthens the neural connections associated with that memory. It is like a path through a forest: the more times we walk it, the clearer it becomes. Re-reading is looking at the path on a map; testing is actually walking it.

Add to this other powerful principles:

  • Desirable difficulties (Robert Bjork): making learning harder — within limits — improves retention. That is why flashcards work better than re-reading: they are harder.
  • Dual coding (Allan Paivio): combining verbal information with visual information (images, diagrams) creates two memory pathways instead of one.
  • Generation effect: information we produce ourselves is better remembered than information we merely read. Making our own flashcards is, in itself, an act of studying.
  • Micro-learning: short, focused sessions (3 to 7 minutes) have a disproportionately high benefit on attention and retention.

Why flashcards are brilliant

Flashcards are not a gimmick. They are a machine for applying cognitive science. A single card simultaneously activates:

  • Active retrieval — seeing the question and trying to recall the answer before flipping
  • Spaced repetition — cards you know well appear less often, cards you get wrong appear more
  • Dual coding — you can include images, diagrams, colours
  • Generation effect — if you make them yourself
  • Micro-learning — you can do 10 cards in the supermarket queue, 15 on the bus, 5 while waiting for your coffee

They are quick to make, fit in your pocket (or on your phone), and turn every dead moment in the day into a study session. A student who does 20 minutes of flashcards a day during "dead time" — without scheduling anything extra — can accumulate over 120 hours of study per year that was previously simply wasted.

When flashcards are BAD study

Now the part that almost nobody mentions. Flashcards can be terrible if used incorrectly. And most people use them incorrectly.

Mistake 1: Putting everything into flashcards

If you try to put all your material into cards, you will end up with hundreds or thousands, and you will need hours a day just to review them. That is brute force, not intelligence. Flashcards should be used for information you cannot fix any other way — facts, definitions, dates, formulae, vocabulary. Material that can be understood as a network (relationships between concepts, reasoning, argumentation) should be studied with other techniques: mind maps, diagrams, teaching someone else.

Mistake 2: Memorising the card instead of the knowledge

After seeing the same flashcard 20 times, the brain learns to recognise the card — the first three words, the formatting, even the position in the list — and not the knowledge. You read "What is the function of the..." and you already know the answer is "mitochondria". But in an exam, the question comes differently, and you freeze. This is called cued recall vs. free recall. It is a dangerous illusion of competence.

Mistake 3: Never merging or levelling up

Flashcards that stay the same for ever test the same thing at the same level. When you have already mastered a fact, that card should be merged with others to create higher-level questions that test relationships between concepts. Example: if you already know "How does protein synthesis work?" and "How are hormones received by cell receptors?", merge them into a question like "How do hormones influence protein synthesis?" This reduces the number of cards and forces higher-order thinking.

Mistake 4: Not investigating the ones you keep getting wrong

If a card comes up wrong three times in a row, there is no point in continuing to repeat it. Stop. Spend 5-10 minutes going deeper: create analogies, link the concept to others you already know, draw a diagram. You are building the knowledge network that was missing — the flashcard alone cannot do that.

How to make flashcards like a professional

Golden rules

  • One concept per card. Never two. Never three. One.
  • Make your own. The act of creating the card is itself study. Pre-made cards are useful as a supplement, never as a substitute.
  • Use images. An anatomy diagram, a photo of a reaction, a graph — visual memory is brutally powerful.
  • Keep them short. If the answer takes 30 seconds to read, split it into two cards.
  • Ask, don't state. "What is the function of the ribosome?" is better than "Ribosome — protein synthesis".
  • Vary the wording. From time to time, rewrite the question differently to avoid memorising the card.

Types of cards

  • Classic question-answer: "What is the capital of Lithuania?" → "Vilnius"
  • Cloze deletion: "Mitosis has {{4}} phases" → shows "Mitosis has ___ phases"
  • Image occlusion: An anatomy image with a structure hidden — ideal for histology, anatomy, geography
  • Reversible: The card works both ways — "English → Portuguese" and "Portuguese → English"
  • Audio: Listen to a word or phrase and try to write/translate it — excellent for languages

Practical examples by subject

Medicine and Health Sciences

Anatomy: Image occlusion on an atlas (e.g. Netter). Cover a muscle, nerve or artery — identify it. Histology: Microscope tissue photo → tissue type + characteristics. Biochemistry: "What is the substrate of hexokinase?" → "Glucose + ATP". Pharmacology: Drug → mechanism of action → side effects (three separate cards, not one).

Younger students (up to secondary school)

Science: "What gas do plants release during photosynthesis?" → "Oxygen (O₂)". History: "In what year was the French Revolution?" → "1789". Languages: Reversible cards with words, idiomatic expressions, irregular verbs. Maths: "Formula for the area of a circle?" → "A = πr²". Geography: Map with a country hidden → identify it.

"Boring" subjects

This is precisely where flashcards shine. Subjects with a lot of pure memorisation — legislation, taxonomy, technical terminology — are perfect for cards. Spaced repetition turns boredom into routine, and routine into mastery. Five minutes a day for a month is worth more than five hours the night before the exam.

Anki: the gold standard (free)

Anki is the most widely used flashcard application in the world. Its spaced repetition algorithm (based on Piotr Wozniak's SM-2) automatically calculates when you should review each card, adapting to your level of mastery.

Where to download

  • Android: AnkiDroid (free)
  • Web: AnkiWeb (free) — works in any browser, syncs with the app
  • iPhone/iPad: AnkiMobile (paid) — if you would rather not pay, just use AnkiWeb in Safari, which is free and works perfectly
  • Windows/Mac/Linux: Anki Desktop (free)

Add-ons worth their weight in gold

Anki has an enormous community of plugins. Some essentials:

  • Image Occlusion Enhanced — creates image occlusion cards directly in Anki
  • Pop-up Wikipedia — instant look-up without leaving the card
  • Review Heatmap — visualise your study consistency over time
  • Frozen Fields — keeps fields fixed between cards (useful when creating several on the same topic)
  • Text to Speech — reads cards aloud, ideal for languages

Ready-made decks to download

No time to make everything from scratch? There are thousands of decks shared by the community:

  • AnkiWeb Shared Decks — the official repository, with decks covering every subject
  • AnkiHub — collaborative decks (highlights for medicine: AnKing Step Deck with 30,000+ cards)
  • Popular decks for languages, sciences, programming, history, music, and much more

A word of caution: ready-made decks are excellent as a supplement. The ideal is always to adapt or create your own, because the act of creation is, in itself, learning.

The revolution: AI + Flashcards

And now we arrive at the present. Because in 2025, making flashcards by hand, card by card, is already optional.

Tools like Google Gemini, ChatGPT, or NotebookLM allow you to take a lecture video, a PDF, your notes, or even a photograph of the whiteboard and transform everything into flashcards automatically. In minutes, not hours.

How it works in practice

  1. Attend the lecture or read the material normally, focusing on overall understanding — without stopping to jot down every detail.
  2. Feed the material to the AI (video, PDF, photo, text). Use a prompt like: "Create flashcards in question|answer format for the key concepts in this material."
  3. The AI generates dozens of cards in seconds, organised, with the key concepts extracted.
  4. Review and edit: Remove the irrelevant ones, rephrase the ones you do not like, add images or diagrams.
  5. Import into Anki (.txt file with | or ; separator) and start studying.

Google Gemini, for example, can literally watch YouTube videos — not merely read the transcript, but analyse diagrams, equations and illustrations in the video. This generates far richer flashcards than any previous tool.

Why this is revolutionary

Before: you attend the lecture, pause every 2 minutes, write notes, create cards. Total time: 3x the duration of the lecture.

Now: you attend the lecture focused on understanding, the AI takes notes for you, and at the end you have the flashcards ready. Total time: 1x the duration of the lecture + 10 minutes of review. You save hours. And the quality of understanding goes up, because you were not constantly interrupting your train of thought to jot things down.

Better still: you can ask the AI to tailor the cards to your specific difficulties. "Make more detailed flashcards about the Krebs cycle, which is where I struggle most." Or: "Rephrase these cards for a 12-year-old." The decks stop being generic and become custom-made.

The complete strategy: how to integrate everything

Here is a system that combines the best of everything we have discussed:

  1. Long study session (weekly):
    • Start by reviewing the cards you keep getting wrong — go deeper, build connections
    • Take the cards you have got right 3 times in a row — merge them into higher-level questions
    • Study new material, using AI to generate the flashcards
    • Review and edit the new cards — adapt them, add images
    • Preview next week's material (this is one of the most important and most neglected steps)
  2. Daily micro-sessions (dead time):
    • Supermarket queue, bus, waiting for coffee, before bed
    • 5 to 15 minutes, 100-150 cards per week — without scheduling anything extra
    • Mark the ones you got right 3 times in a row and the ones you got wrong 3 times

This cycle, repeated week after week, creates a compound interest effect on knowledge. Each week builds on the previous one. Within a few weeks, the difference compared to traditional studying is staggering.

Final note

Flashcards are not a fad. They are the practical translation of over 140 years of research into human memory. From Ebbinghaus in 1885 to modern spaced repetition algorithms, through the AI revolution — this is a tool that never stops improving.

The secret is not in the cards themselves. It is in the discipline of using them correctly: few but good, adapted, with images, made by you (or by the AI and edited by you), reviewed at increasing intervals, and merged as your mastery grows.

Simple. Scientific. Free. And within reach of anyone with a phone.
